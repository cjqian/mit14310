Existing data libraries

Online collections

  • Census/government data dumps
  • Harvard/MIT datasets
  • Amazon dataverse
  • DHS (deomgraphic & health surveys)
  • LSMS (living standards measurement surveys); comprehensive code books & interviews
  • Rand, similar to LSMS
    • Surveys families in countries over many years

RCT data

  • Stored in HQSS dataverse
  • Often more rich than just answering targeted question
  • Less used by researchers

Standards

  • ICPSR public data standards:
    • No identifiable information
    • Requires ocumentatation
  • American Economic Association requires journals to publish data for research (not the most clean)
  • Most data can be used as long as it’s cited

Internet resources

  • 538, Yahoo news, Uber movement
  • Wayback machine

Data that is not readily available

  • Restricted by cost:
    • Libraries might have access
    • Commercial data might be available for research
  • Restricted by confidentiality (NDA)
  • Restricted locations in which to access data
  • Manual harvesting/cleaning

Harvesting data

  • Web scraping; scraping unfinished datasets from the internet
    • Pull data from/crawl websites, forms
    • APIs (application program interface)
      • Could be free, trialed or require authorization
  • Collecting your own data

Example: Using Google Map’s API

Background:

  • Usually used by those building smartphone apps/websites
  • Designed for commercial applications with cost structure
  • Two types of queries:
    1. Departure time now (based on crowdsourced + historical data for a route)
    2. Departure time future (predict based on historical data)
    • No direct access to historical data
  • Gives CSV result

Example: Delhi Odd-Even study

Delhi is very polluted and experimented by restricting number plates’ ability to drive on different days (odd-even).

  • Unlike Mexico (all the time), this is implemented only during periods of peak pollution

  • Use maps API to see how long it takes to drive 93 routes during a given time

    • Sunday has less traffic
    • Shows that policy did decrease travel time in locations with implemented policy
      • Could suggest success; most pollution comes from idling

Collecting your own data

  • Need to do an IRB application (Internal Review Board) beforehand to determine ethics; possibly create consent forms
  • Use survey tools (websites, apps, Amazon MTurk)

Steps:
* Obtain funding
* Prepare data management plan (sharing, privacy)
* Obtain Human Subject Approval (IRB)
* Design/pilot data collection instrument

Human subjects in research

Background on regulation protecting human subjects

  • Nazi research
  • Tuskegee Syphilis trials 1932-1972
    • No treatment on unknowing/lied to impoverished sharecroppers
    • Many dead and infected
    • Also, didn’t yield any scientific results

Belmont Report (Title 45 Part 46)

  • Designed for biomedical research

What is a human subject?

Definitions:

  • Research: A systematic investiagion to develop/contribute to generalizable knowledge
    • Excludes ungeneralizable* knowledge (companies experimenting without wanting to publish)
  • Human subject: Living individual about whom an investigator can conduct research or obtain identifiable data
    • Require permission if you either collect your own data or use data with identifiers

The Belmont Principles

  1. Respect for persons
    • Informed consent
      • Exception: collecting consent would hurt the study, and there is no harm
      • Psych study with deception requires debriefing after
    • Protecting privacy, maintaining confidentiality
    • Safeguards for vulnerable/coercive subjects
  2. Beneficence
    • Maximize benefits, reduce harm
    • Can’t overcompensate people to perform dangerous tasks
  3. Justice
    • Justify human subject population makeup
