This lecture focuses on exploratory data analysis; data visualization can be used for exploratory analysis or to convey stories to others.

The histogram

  • Rough estimate of the probability distribution function of a continuous variable, obtained by binning and counting observations
  • Can represent counts or densities (divided by overall population)

Plotting histograms in R

  • Conventions: have a data/ and a graph/ subdirectory
  • A tibble is a TidyVerse table/dataframe
  • plot_grid by cowplot can plot multiple grids

What’s the optimal bin shape?

  • More observations = more noise/bumpiness
  • Tradeoff between bias and variance; information relayed vs. noise tolerance

Advantages of data cleaning in statistical software:

  • Track record of changes
  • No overwriting the underlying data

Intro to kernel density estimation

  • A kernel density estimation (KDE) is a smoothed histogram, another PDF visualization
  • Extension of histogram: at each point, we take a weighted average of the frequency of th eobservations
  • Works for any distribution centered on a point; integrates to 1
    • Typicall use normal/Gaussian or Epanechnikov kernel function
  • Difference: histograms use bin width (graphed in groups of size xxx); KDEs use band width
  • We assume an i.i.d. sample; no assumptions about the shape of the distribution
  • Kernels estimate density, not counts

Kernel density estimation in R

  • Default geom_histogram() is a count histogram w/ frequency on the y-axis; to specify history, aes() must contain density

Bandwidth in kernel functions

  • There’s bias at the boundary of the estimation; you only have half of the data (very apparent in large bandwidth)
  • The bigger the bandwidth, the smoother the kernel (tradeoff between overfitting/variance and oversmoothing/bias)

Comparing distributions

  • Binomial distribution $B(n, p) is approximately normal with mean npnpnp and variance np(1−p)np(1-p)np(1−p) for large nnn and for ppp not too close to 0 or 1

Plotting cumulative distribution functions

  • CDF is the integral of density
  • Sometimes better for comparing two distributions (can immediately determine heights; easier than comparing areas)
  • First order stochastic dominance: for any x-value; one distribution has higher probability than another
