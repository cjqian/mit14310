Independence of random variables

Can you construct the joint distribution knowing only the marginals? No; we need the relationship between the random variables as well.

XXX and YYY are independent if P(X∪A and Y∪B)=P(X∪A)P(Y∪B)∀A,BP(X\cup A \text{ and } Y\cup B) = P(X\cup A)P(Y\cup B) \forall A, BP(X∪A and Y∪B)=P(X∪A)P(Y∪B)∀A,B, which implies that the joint CDF of two random variables is the product of the marginal CDFs. So, if you can put the joint distribution into this form, they’re independent:

Fxy(x,y)=Fx(x)Fy(y)F_{xy}(x, y) = F_x(x)F_y(y)Fxy​(x,y)=Fx​(x)Fy​(y)

xxx and yyy are independent if the joint PDF factors as the two marginal PDFs.

Example of independent random variables

Recall example: fxy(x,y)=cx2y for x2≥y≥1f_{xy}(x, y) = cx^2y \text{ for } x^2 \geq y \geq 1fxy​(x,y)=cx2y for x2≥y≥1

Are they independent?

  • The support implies that values of the random variable of yyy can depend on the value of xxx and vice-versa, so no.

For discrete random variables, if you have a table representing their joint probability distribution, the variables are independent iff the rows and columns are proportional (multiples of the others).

Conditional distributions

Conditional distributions allow the updating of a random variable.

The conditional PDF of Y given X is the joint PDF divided by the marginal PDF:

fy∣x(y,x)=fxy(x,y)fx(x)f_{y|x}(y,x) = \frac{f_{xy}(x, y)}{f_x(x)}fy∣x​(y,x)=fx​(x)fxy​(x,y)​

For discrete: $ = P(Y=y|X=x)$

(If variables are independent, this is just the product.)
